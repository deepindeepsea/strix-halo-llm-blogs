A field series · Results · Part 2

We loaded a 120-billion-parameter model onto a single integrated GPU. No discrete card. No reboot. It answered in real time.

Part 2 of the Strix Halo field series — the memory frontier. In Part 1 we proved a 70B dense model fits the iGPU and decodes at ~5 tok/s. Now we go bigger and smarter: gpt-oss-120B, a Mixture-of-Experts model in MXFP4, 59 GiB of weights streamed straight into the 96 GiB unified-memory pool. It decodes at ~56 tok/s for a single user — an order of magnitude faster than the 70B dense — because only ~5B of those 120B parameters move per token. Here is every number, both backends, and how to reproduce it.

AMD Field AI Enthusiast · by Pradeep Nallimelli · June 2026 · status: RESULTS — measured on real hardware

The one-paragraph verdict

A 116.8-billion-parameter model (gpt-oss-120B, MXFP4 MoE, ~5.1B active) loads entirely onto the Radeon 8060S iGPU at -ngl 99 — 59 GiB of weights inside the 96 GiB GTT pool, with no BIOS edit and no reboot. Single-user decode is ~56 tok/s (Vulkan, the better single-user backend here), and Vulkan prefills 3.2× faster than ROCm (569 vs 179 tok/s). But under concurrency the story flips earlier than it did for the 30B: ROCm overtakes Vulkan on aggregate decode at just 4 streams, and at 16 streams ROCm hits 227 tok/s — +78% over Vulkan, which plateaus. The rule sharpens: Vulkan for interactive, ROCm for batch — and the crossover comes sooner the bigger the MoE. Same llama.cpp build (063d9c1), no per-backend cherry-picking.

Why a 120B on an APU is the interesting problem

A discrete 24 GB or even 32 GB GPU simply cannot hold this model. A 120B even in 4-bit is ~60 GB of weights — it would have to be split across multiple cards or spilled to CPU, and either path collapses throughput. The Strix Halo bet is different: one big pool of unified memory, and an iGPU allowed to address almost all of it.

The constraintThe Strix Halo answer
Weights are 59 GiB — bigger than any consumer discrete VRAM.128 GB unified LPDDR5X; the iGPU draws from a 96 GiB GTT pool, so 59 GiB fits with ~37 GiB to spare for KV cache.
120B dense would decode at ~3 tok/s on ~256 GB/s — unusable.gpt-oss-120B is MoE: only ~5.1B parameters activate per token, so the bytes-moved-per-token is small → ~56 tok/s.
Big-model loading usually needs GTT/grub tuning or a reboot.At 59 GiB the model fits the default 96 GiB pool — no reboot, no grub edit, no BIOS carveout change. It just loads.

The headline isn’t the size — it’s the combination. 120B parameters of knowledge capacity, at the decode speed of a ~5B model, on a single power-sipping APU. MoE + MXFP4 + unified memory is the trifecta that makes it work.

Get the model and load it (the exact commands):

# gpt-oss-120B MXFP4 GGUF — three shards, ~63 GB total download
BASE=https://huggingface.co/ggml-org/gpt-oss-120b-GGUF/resolve/main
for i in 00001 00002 00003; do
  wget -c "$BASE/gpt-oss-120b-mxfp4-${i}-of-00003.gguf"
done

# Multi-part GGUF auto-loads from shard 00001 — point llama.cpp at part 1 only.
# All layers on the iGPU; no reboot, no grub change needed at 59 GiB.
build-vulkan/bin/llama-server \
  -m gpt-oss-120b-mxfp4-00001-of-00003.gguf -ngl 99 -fa on -c 8192

Confirm your llama.cpp supports it: the source must contain LLM_ARCH_OPENAI_MOE "gpt-oss" and GGML_TYPE_MXFP4. Commit 063d9c1 does. -ngl 99 places all layers on the GPU; the 59 GiB load takes ~30–60 s the first time.

Result 2 · flash attention — this time, on wins everywhere

A notable departure from Part 1. There, ROCm prefill was faster with FA off on the Qwen3-30B. For gpt-oss-120B MXFP4, FA on is the best setting on both backends, for both prefill and decode.

BackendFA off prefillFA on prefillFA off decodeFA on decodeBest setting
ROCm177.0179.149.250.9FA on
Vulkan524.6568.954.555.8FA on

The lesson: flash-attention tuning is model-architecture-specific, not just backend-specific. The “ROCm prefills faster with FA off” rule from Part 1 was true for that model — it does not carry over to this MXFP4 MoE. Always re-sweep -fa 0,1 when you change model family. Here the answer is simple: leave FA on.

Result 3 · the crossover — concurrency flips the winner at 4 streams

Driving the 120B with llama-batched-bench at 1–16 parallel sequences (128-token prompt + 128-token gen each, FA on) is where the operational decision lives. Two views matter: aggregate decode (token-generation throughput) and total throughput (prefill + decode combined).

Vulkan leads at 1–2 streams, but ROCm overtakes at 4 and never looks back. By 16 streams ROCm reaches 226.7 tok/s while Vulkan has plateaued and regressed to 127.1 — a 78% ROCm advantage. Note this crossover is earlier than the Qwen3-30B in Part 1 (which crossed at 16): the bigger the MoE, the sooner ROCm’s batched scheduler pulls ahead under load.

This is the nuance worth internalizing: which backend “wins” under concurrency depends on what you measure. If your workload is decode-dominated (long generations, short prompts — chat, agents), ROCm is the batch winner from 4 streams up. If it’s prefill-heavy (long prompts, short answers — RAG, classification, summarization), Vulkan’s 3× prefill keeps its total throughput ahead even at 16 streams. Pick by workload shape, not by a single headline number.

The decision, stated plainly: single user → Vulkan. Decode-bound batch serving (≥4 streams) → ROCm. Prefill-bound batch → Vulkan. The 120B doesn’t change the Part 1 rule — it sharpens it and moves the decode crossover down to 4 streams.

Result 4 · the capacity picture — what fits, and what’s left over

The 59 GiB of weights is only part of the budget. The 96 GiB GTT pool also has to hold the KV cache, and that leftover is what determines how much context you can serve.

59 < 96, so the default pool holds it. The reboot/grub-tuning gate only appears for the even larger 235B-class models.

Compared to Part 1’s 70B-dense (39.6 GiB, ~5 tok/s), this 120B-MoE is 50% more weight on disk but ~11× faster to decode. That is the entire argument for MoE on bandwidth-limited silicon, stated as one comparison: capacity is set by total parameters; speed is set by active parameters. Strix Halo’s unified memory lets you buy the first without paying for it in the second.

Reproduce every number

The exact sweep, runnable on any Strix Halo box once both backends are built (see Part 1 for the build commands):

MODEL=gpt-oss-120b/gpt-oss-120b-mxfp4-00001-of-00003.gguf   # shard 1 auto-loads the set
ROCM=llama.cpp/build/bin
VK=llama.cpp/build-vulkan/bin

# Result 1 + 2 — single-user FA sweep (prefill + decode), both backends
$ROCM/llama-bench -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa 0,1 -p 512 -n 128 -r 3
$VK/llama-bench   -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa 0,1 -p 512 -n 128 -r 3

# Result 3 — concurrency sweep (note: newer llama.cpp needs '-fa on', not bare '-fa')
$ROCM/llama-batched-bench -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa on -c 4608 -npp 128 -ntg 128 -npl 1,2,4,8,16
$VK/llama-batched-bench   -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa on -c 4608 -npp 128 -ntg 128 -npl 1,2,4,8,16

Environment: Ryzen AI Max+ 395 / Radeon 8060S (gfx1151), 128 GB unified, 96 GiB GTT; Ubuntu 24.04.4, kernel 6.17-oem; ROCm 7.2.0; llama.cpp 063d9c1. Model 59.02 GiB, 116.83 B params, MXFP4 MoE. Raw llama-bench / llama-batched-bench tables and CSV/JSON are archived alongside this post.
