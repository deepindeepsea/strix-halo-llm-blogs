A field series · Results · Part 2.5

Then we doubled it. A 235-billion-parameter model, 96 GiB of weights, running on a single integrated GPU.

Part 2.5 of the Strix Halo field series — past the default pool. In Part 2 a 120B-MoE fit the stock 96 GiB GTT pool with no reboot. This time the model doesn’t fit — Qwen3-235B-A22B in UD-Q3_K_XL is 96.59 GiB of weights, so we cross the reboot gate: a one-line grub change opens a 120 GiB GTT pool, and 235 billion parameters load entirely onto the Radeon 8060S iGPU. It decodes at ~18 tok/s for a single user — and along the way we hit a memory-pressure trap that silently turns the benchmark into a disk-speed test. Here is every number, both backends, the reboot tuning, and the one flag that makes it all reproducible.

AMD Field AI Enthusiast · by Pradeep Nallimelli · June 2026 · status: RESULTS — measured on real hardware

The one-paragraph verdict

A 235.09-billion-parameter model (Qwen3-235B-A22B, Q3_K MoE, ~22B active) loads entirely onto the Radeon 8060S iGPU at -ngl 99 — 96.59 GiB of weights inside a 120 GiB GTT pool that we opened with a single grub line and one reboot. Single-user decode is ~18 tok/s (Vulkan, the better single-user backend here); ROCm prefills fastest (168 tok/s, 1.18× Vulkan) — the prefill crown returns to ROCm FA-off, unlike the 120B. Under concurrency Vulkan leads aggregate throughput up to 8 streams, then ROCm takes over at 16 streams: 64.2 tok/s total — +56% as Vulkan’s decode collapses. And one hard-won methodology note: at 96.59 GiB on a 125 GiB box, you must disable mmap, or the GPU silently re-reads weights from NVMe on every pass and you measure your SSD instead of your iGPU. Same llama.cpp build (063d9c1), no per-backend cherry-picking.

Why a 235B on an APU is the frontier

Part 2 fit a 120B because 59 GiB slipped under the default 96 GiB GTT pool. A 235B at Q3_K is 96.59 GiB — it does not fit the default pool. This is the reboot gate Part 2 promised, and crossing it is the whole point of this entry.

The constraintThe Strix Halo answer
Weights are 96.59 GiB — larger than the stock 96 GiB GTT pool.Raise the TTM page limit via grub (ttm.pages_limit / ttm.page_pool_size) and reboot once → a 120 GiB pool; ROCm then reports 122880 MiB of usable VRAM.
235B dense would decode at ~1–2 tok/s — unusable.Qwen3-235B-A22B is MoE: only ~22B parameters activate per token, so bytes-moved-per-token stays bounded → ~18 tok/s.
A model this close to total RAM thrashes. 96.59 GiB is ~77% of the 125 GiB box.Disable mmap (--mmap 0 / --no-mmap) so weights load once into the GTT pool instead of re-faulting from disk every compute pass (see §5).

The headline isn’t just the size — it’s that an integrated GPU is now addressing nearly 100 GiB of model weights. 235B of knowledge capacity, at the decode speed of a ~22B model, on a single power-sipping APU. MoE + low-bit quant + a tuned unified-memory pool is what puts a frontier-class model on a laptop-class chip.

The bench · one APU, two backends, same commit

Identical method to Parts 1–2: nothing differs except the GPU backend. Both binaries compiled from the same llama.cpp checkout (063d9c1) on the same machine; only the backend flag changed. The two new variables this round are the GTT reboot tuning and mmap disabled.

Open the 120 GiB pool (the reboot gate), then load the model:

# 1) Raise the GTT (TTM) page pool. 31457280 pages x 4 KiB = 120 GiB.
#    Edit /etc/default/grub, append to GRUB_CMDLINE_LINUX_DEFAULT:
#      ttm.pages_limit=31457280 ttm.page_pool_size=31457280
sudo update-grub && sudo reboot          # one reboot — required at this size

# 2) Verify the pool is open (should print 122880 MiB / 120 GiB):
rocminfo | grep -i gfx1151
build/bin/llama-bench --help >/dev/null   # device line shows VRAM: 122880 MiB

# 3) Download the 3-shard UD-Q3_K_XL GGUF (~104 GB) and load shard 1.
#    NOTE the mandatory --mmap 0 — see section 5.
build-vulkan/bin/llama-server \
  -m Qwen3-235B-A22B-UD-Q3_K_XL-00001-of-00003.gguf -ngl 99 -fa on --no-mmap -c 8192

The grub change persists across reboots; on a headless box it is worth pinning a static IP so the machine comes back reachable. No BIOS carveout edit is needed — the GTT pool is the free, reversible tuning lever.

Three models, three different FA verdicts — proof that you must re-sweep -fa 0,1 every time you change model family.

For Qwen3-235B, ROCm prefills fastest with FA off (168 vs 145) but decodes best with FA on (16.0 vs 15.1) — a within-backend split. Vulkan prefers FA on for both. Compare the series: Part 1 (Qwen3-30B) → ROCm prefill FA-off; Part 2 (gpt-oss-120B MXFP4) → FA-on everywhere; Part 2.5 (Qwen3-235B Q3_K) → back to ROCm prefill FA-off. The flag is model-architecture-specific, full stop.

Driving the 235B with llama-batched-bench at 1–16 parallel sequences (128-token prompt + 128-token gen each, FA on, no-mmap). Two views matter: aggregate decode (token-generation throughput) and total throughput (prefill + decode combined).

The crossover lands at 16 — later than the 120B (which crossed at 4). The bigger, heavier Q3_K experts keep Vulkan competitive longer at low concurrency, but ROCm’s batched scheduler is the only one that still scales when the box is saturated.

Total throughput tells the same story cleanly: Vulkan owns 1–8 streams, ROCm owns 16+. At full saturation ROCm delivers 64.2 vs 41.0 tok/s — +56%. Unlike the 120B (where Vulkan’s 3× prefill kept its total ahead even at 16), here ROCm’s modest prefill edge plus its decode scaling give it the high-concurrency total outright.

The decision, stated plainly: single user or light load (≤8 streams) → Vulkan (best decode, best total). Saturated batch serving (≥16 streams) → ROCm (+56% total, +66% decode). Fastest single-user prefill → ROCm FA-off. The Part 1 rule holds — Vulkan for interactive, ROCm for batch — the crossover just moves with model size and quant.

This is the inflection the whole series was building toward. Part 1 proved a 70B-dense fits and crawls (~5 tok/s). Part 2 proved a 120B-MoE fits the default pool and flies (~56 tok/s). Part 2.5 proves the biggest open-weight MoE of its class — 235B — fits a tuned pool and stays interactive (~18 tok/s). The ceiling on this APU isn’t the GPU; it’s how much of the 125 GiB you’re willing to hand the GTT pool.

Result 5 · the trap — how a 96 GiB model turns your benchmark into a disk test

This is the most important reproducibility lesson in the series. At 96.59 GiB — ~77% of the 125 GiB box — llama.cpp’s default mmap silently destroys your results.

Symptom (default mmap)CauseFix (mmap off)
A single bench stage takes 13+ minutes with no result; GPU shows 100% but nothing finishes.The GTT allocation is file-backed. Under memory pressure the page cache can’t retain it, so the GPU re-reads weights from NVMe on every compute pass.Stage finishes in ~2 minutes. Weights load once into RAM/GTT and stay there.
Steady ~310 MB/s disk read during “compute”; process stuck in Dl (uninterruptible I/O) state.You are measuring your SSD’s sequential read, not the iGPU’s matmul.Disk read drops to ~0 MB/s; process state becomes Rl (running).

The rule, generalized: on any Strix Halo / unified-memory APU, when a model is larger than ~50% of total RAM, always disable mmap — --mmap 0 for llama-bench, --no-mmap for llama-batched-bench and llama-server. Verify it worked by confirming disk read ≈ 0 and process state Rl (not Dl) after the model loads.

Every number in this post was collected with mmap off. Without this flag the entire 235B campaign would have reported the speed of an NVMe drive.

Reproduce every number

The exact sweep, runnable on any Strix Halo box once the 120 GiB pool is open and both backends are built (see Part 1 for the build commands):

MODEL=Qwen3-235B-A22B/UD-Q3_K_XL/Qwen3-235B-A22B-UD-Q3_K_XL-00001-of-00003.gguf   # shard 1 auto-loads the set
ROCM=llama.cpp/build/bin
VK=llama.cpp/build-vulkan/bin

# Result 1 + 2 — single-user FA sweep (prefill + decode), both backends. NOTE --mmap 0.
$ROCM/llama-bench -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa 0,1 --mmap 0 -p 512 -n 128 -r 3
$VK/llama-bench   -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa 0,1 --mmap 0 -p 512 -n 128 -r 3

# Result 3 — concurrency sweep (newer llama.cpp needs '-fa on'; --no-mmap is mandatory here)
$ROCM/llama-batched-bench -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa on --no-mmap -c 4608 -npp 128 -ntg 128 -npl 1,2,4,8,16
$VK/llama-batched-bench   -m $MODEL -ngl 99 -fa on --no-mmap -c 4608 -npp 128 -ntg 128 -npl 1,2,4,8,16

Environment: Ryzen AI Max+ 395 / Radeon 8060S (gfx1151), 128 GB unified (125 GiB usable), 120 GiB GTT via ttm.pages_limit=31457280 ttm.page_pool_size=31457280 + reboot; Ubuntu 24.04.4, kernel 6.17.0-1012-oem; ROCm 7.2.0; llama.cpp 063d9c1.
